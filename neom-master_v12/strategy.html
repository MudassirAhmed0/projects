ATTACKS TYPES

THREAT LANDSCAPE

Having outlined the growing sophistication of attacks, we now move on to the threat landscape that is relevant to NEOM. NEOM may be susceptible to a large number of cyber threats due to the wide attack surface of connected devices required to enable a cognitive city.

01 02 03 04 05 06
01

Critical National Infrastructure (CNI)

Attacks that target the critical infrastructure systems like those driving power generation, water treatment, electricity production and other platforms that are interconnected to form the energy “grid”. CNI also includes telecom infrastructure, health care systems and transport; that if disrupted will cause a physical incident and even impact the safety of humans. ​​

​NEOM will have CNI across most of the 14 sectors and leverage technology at the forefront of digital enablement. These CNI assets would be targeted by cyber attackers to compromise security across the cognitive city.

02

Emerging Technology Attacks

This type of attack leverages emerging and disruptive technologies such as AI, machine learning, blockchain etc. Defending against these types of attacks is increasingly complex.

Recent attacks have used machine learning to mimic the usual digital activity of users, to go undetected and even cover the tracks of perpetrators.

Given NEOM’s prominence and scale, sophisticated threat actors might employ attacks using emerging technology.​

03

Big Game Ransomware

Organized threat actors have shifted their ransomware focus from individuals to high-value targets. It is estimated that ransomware groups generated up to US$20b in revenue in 2020. Ransomware also has the ability to traverse between networks and affect other targets, causing the destruction of software and data. ​​

NEOM being a cognitive city powered by data will be a target for such attacks as they will be seen as a wealthy target that will pay a huge ransom to recover from an outage or to prevent any negative brand image impacts.

04

Supply Chain Risk

Supply chain attacks have been a concern for cybersecurity experts because the chain reaction triggered by one attack on a single supplier can compromise a network of providers. In the cyber supply chain, risks may include the introduction of counterfeits, illegal manufacture, tampering, theft, the introduction of harmful software and hardware, and poor manufacturing and development processes. ​ ​​

​NEOM employs many third parties and will continue to have a complex supply chain ecosystem, which will add complexity in defending from such attacks.​

05

Data and Privacy Breaches

When someone has unauthorized access to information, this is referred to as a privacy breach. NEOM has and will hold personal information such as names, addresses, identification numbers, credit card numbers and other sensitive data of its own citizens, visitors, suppliers, etc.​

NEOM may be targeted by threat actors for either financial gain or intelligence collection by gaining access to this information. Furthermore, there are international and local regulations that include penalties for data and privacy breaches.​

06

Financial Attacks

Large scale cyber heists, in recent times, have demonstrated how weaknesses that exist in payment systems can be exploited by cyber criminals.

​NEOM will have financial systems that will be of interest to threat actors, and such attacks will have both a financial impact, but also one of reputation and brand degradation.​

BENCHMARKING OUTCOMES

The outcomes of the benchmarking exercise were divided into three themes

governance

Cyber as Top Priority in Digital Agenda

Cybersecurity is positioned as a top priority in countries’ national digital agenda and receives a significant amount of backing from governmental institutions in the form of dedicated budgets and well-defined security strategies.​

National Cybersecurity Strategy

A national cybersecurity strategy is defined across all sectors to support national security, economic development and includes action plans or a roadmap for enhancing identified improvement areas.

strategy

Evolving National Cybersecurity

The national cybersecurity strategy is periodically updated based on the growing threat environment and input is obtained from subject matter experts who provide an overview of the threat landscape and potential mitigation actions.​​

Information Sharing

Coordination and data sharing for the purpose of protection and detection is encouraged by the governmental institution responsible for cybersecurity. Information sharing ensures all parties involved in the cybersecurity program are aware of potential threats and can proactively implement remediation actions.​​

All-inclusive Regulatory Framework

All countries have adopted a national cybersecurity regulatory framework. Generally, the framework covers a variety of domains that are essential for maintaining a cyber-secure organization. The compliance to the framework is periodically monitored using various tools and techniques.​

Centralized Authority, Federated Operations

Many countries have evolved towards a centralized authority with federated operations, allowing sector specific expertise and technical know-how to have a say in all matters concerning cybersecurity.

governance

National Incident Response Team

All countries have created a national incident response function that is responsible for responding to large-scale security incidents of national significance. The involvement of the national incident response function is based on a classification schema of incidents ​​

Cybercrime

Several laws have been enacted to enforce cybersecurity regulations and protect its citizens from cybercrime. The cyber laws are substantive in nature and protect residents when they indulge in online activities.​

Protection of Critical National Infrastructure

Attention to the protection of CNIs from cyber- attacks has increased. The focus on protection of CNIs has increased due to the number of cyber- attacks targeting such critical assets. Some countries have started to adopt CNI specific entities and regulations for focused protection of such assets.

Cybersecurity Budget

The national budget for cybersecurity initiatives and the industry cybersecurity spending to protect OT has increased as governments have started to realize the potential damage that can be caused by a single attack on a critical asset.

operations

Cyber Human Capital Capability

Investment in building local cybersecurity expertise has increased and governments have implemented various programs to enhance the knowledge base within their respective regions, regarding cybersecurity. Programs are generally focused on training students and young professionals in the field of cybersecurity and ensuring the future generations are well-equipped to deal with an advancing threat landscape.​​​

Active Defense

Countries with offensive cyber capabilities have aligned that role to national defense entities.​ ​​

Relationships

This pillar focuses on forming partnerships with external organizations to gain the best possible insights into rising threats and state-of-the-art defenses against them. It also lets us foster an ecosystem where trust is a feature and a requirement for participating organizations.

  • Build collaborative partnerships with external organizations, enabling NEOM to gain best possible insights into emerging threats and state of the art capabilities to defend against them
  • Foster an ecosystem where trust is a characteristic and a requirement for all organizations and entities that participate with the NEOM environment
Empowerment

This pillar focuses on how we can attract the best cybersecurity talent from around the world and build local expertise to reduce leakage in the economy. Furthermore, it helps establish NEOM as a leading incubator of security startup companies, allowing it to drive and benefit from emerging and established technologies.

  • Attract the world’s brightest cybersecurity talent and nurture home-grown expertise within NEOM; thereby reducing leakage in the economy​
  • Establish NEOM as crucible for new venture community backed security company development, allowing it to both drive and take advantage of leading-edge innovation​
Acceleration

This pillar focuses on how we will establish a digital utopia in which city life is woven with a secure by conceptualization approach. In addition, it will permit NEOM sectors to utilize the cutting-edge modern cyber infrastructure and digital platforms for effective governance and management.

  • Accelerate the development of a digital utopia where a secure by conceptualization approach is woven into the fabric or DNA of city life​
  • Enable state-of-the-art modern cyber infrastructure that extensively uses digital platforms for effective governance and management across NEOM sectors​
Legislation

This pillar focuses on how we will leverage current legislation to put safety, security and privacy at the heart of NEOM's life. Furthermore, we must devise a coordinated and concerted response to the cybercrime problem that leverages the latest technological advances. ​

  • Leverage state of the art legislation to ensure safety, security and privacy are woven into the fabric of NEOM life​.
  • Address and counter the rise of cyber-crime through a concerted and coordinated approach that leverages the world’s most innovative processes and technologies​
Innovation

This pillar focuses on how our strategic objective will be to advance industry practices and approaches to leverage disruptive technologies like quantum computing, artificial intelligence and 6G for cybersecurity controls. We will also create a world center of creative and audacious thinkers to enable the world's most cyber-secure environment. In addition, our objective will also include empowering sectors to develop intellectual property that is enabled by trust and resilience.​

  • Leapfrog current industry practice and approaches to use disruptive technology (e.g., quantum computing, AI, 6G) for the purposes of enabling cybersecurity controls
  • Provide a world center for creative and audacious thinkers to enable the world’s most cyber-secure environment​
  • Empower sectors to develop intellectual property that is enabled by trust and resilience​
Sustainability

This pillar focuses on how NEOM needs to maintain a sustainable cyber landscape, proactively monitoring the environment and ensuring it can survive cyber-attacks and respond to disruptions.​

Maintain a sustainable cyber landscape that provides long-term value by proactively monitoring the environment and ensure NEOM has the ability to respond to disruptions and sustain operations in the face of cyber-attacks​

Excellence

This pillar focuses on how NEOM will establish itself as a global leader in security excellence, influencing how others approach the security challenge. In addition to becoming a thought leader in cybersecurity, NEOM will grow global outreach, raise cybersecurity awareness, and develop a top-notch cybersecurity talent pool.​

  • Establish NEOM as the global center of security excellence – influencing the approach others take to addressing the security challenge​
  • Build operational excellence in cybersecurity by generating thought leadership, expanding global outreach, raising cybersecurity awareness and cultivating a best-in-class cybersecurity talent pool
Digital

This pillar focuses on how we will accelerate approaches to identifying and managing cyber threats like never before, increasing efficiency and streamlining cybersecurity activities.​

  • Leverage digital solutions to accelerate approaches to identifying and managing cyber threats at a level of precision and scale that is unprecedented ​
  • Increase efficiency and streamline cybersecurity activities through digital​

IMPLEMENTATION PLAN

In order to successfully achieve the NEOM cybersecurity strategy, a detailed roadmap and milestones have been developed.

​The implementation of the strategy will be carried out over three phases called waves starting in 2022 through 2030.

2022 - 2024

FIRST WAVE
BUILD
1/3
1.

Creation of regulations, strategies, policies,standards, procedures, and controls

2.

Identification of resource gaps

3.

Mobilization of a team of internal and external skilled resources to act as an enabler for digitalization in NEOM.

4.

Leveraging alliances and partnerships to rapidly upscale the cybersecurity program as required

2025 - 2027

SECOND WAVE
PROPLE
2/3

The Propel wave is focused on building on the foundations in Wave 1 and investing in research, development and innovation, for governing and managing cybersecurity operations. NEOM will also leverage trusted partner(s) to implement solutions throughout NEOM infrastructure, perform security operations, upscale skills and process efficiencies to support ongoing cyber capabilities.​

2028 - 2030

THIRD WAVE
EXCEL
3/3

The Excel wave continues the momentum from the previous wave and leverages state of the art legislation to ensure safety, security and privacy are woven into the fabric of the NEOM life.  This includes establishment of a cybersecurity center of excellence that catalyzes innovation, entrepreneurship, capability building and advises others on best practices for managing cybersecurity.

Lack of talent

There is a global shortage of over 3M open cybersecurity roles and the increased regulations and demand will continue to strain availability of qualified cybersecurity resources.​

​NEOM will need to invest in development, recruiting and retention programs to address the challenges posed by this risk.

Reliability of supply chain

NEOM will be heavily depending on key suppliers and partners to execute the NEOM cybersecurity strategy.​

Continued challenges from the pandemic, geopolitical conditions, regulatory reforms and other factors can potentially disrupt, or delay execution as defined in the implementation plan.​Continued challenges from the pandemic, geopolitical conditions, regulatory reforms and other factors can potentially disrupt, or delay execution as defined in the implementation plan.​

Rapid technology deployment

Rapid technology deployment and adoption of amateur emerging technologies might result in inability to perform security verifications and validations prior to usage in production.

​Also, disruptive technologies can introduce new attack vectors or challenges that have not previously been encountered.​
